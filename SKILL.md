# Fck Paper

Turn rigorous manuscript criticism into a high-aggression private roast without sacrificing factual accuracy.

## Workflow

1. Read the manuscript or the available extracted evidence before judging it. Use the relevant document skill when a PDF, DOCX, or other artifact is involved.
2. Identify the paper's central promise. Prefer the contribution emphasized in the title, abstract, introduction, and conclusion over the flashiest model name.
3. Build the factual spine before adding attitude:
   - What does the paper claim?
   - What experiment would be required to support that claim?
   - What did the authors actually test?
   - Which inputs, assumptions, baselines, or evaluation choices contradict the claimed use case?
   - Which conclusions exceed the evidence?
4. Rank the failures by importance. Lead with a broken claim-method-evidence chain, leakage, invalid task definition, unavailable inputs, or unsupported generalization. Treat weak prose and ugly figures as secondary unless they obstruct scientific interpretation.
5. Deliver the roast at the intensity requested by the user. Use profanity only when explicitly requested or when the user invokes this skill for that purpose.
6. End with a compact verdict explaining whether the paper needs revision, experimental redesign, or rejection.

## Rhetorical Structure

Use this sequence when enough evidence exists:

1. **Opening verdict:** One or two blunt sentences that name the overall failure.
2. **Collapsed central claim:** Contrast what the paper promises with what it actually tests.
3. **Internal contradictions:** Expose unavailable inputs, causal violations, leakage, circular validation, or task-definition confusion.
4. **Novelty and method:** Separate a genuinely new capability from routine component assembly.
5. **Evidence quality:** Attack missing controls, ablations, repeated runs, uncertainty, baselines, and reproducibility details.
6. **Presentation:** Note padding, repeated plots, terminology abuse, or careless factual errors.
7. **Final sentence:** Give a memorable but evidence-grounded rejection or redesign verdict.

Prefer sharp contrast constructions such as:

- "The paper claims X, but the experiment only demonstrates Y."
- "This is not weak evidence; it is evidence for a different task."
- "The contribution exists in the adjectives, not in the experimental design."
- "The figures multiply while the unanswered question stays exactly where it was."
- "This is component assembly presented as methodological invention."

Adapt these patterns to the actual manuscript. Do not repeat canned lines when they are not supported.

## Tone Controls

- **Direct:** Severe but professional; no profanity.
- **Brutal:** Mock the paper's logic and inflated claims with vivid language.
- **Profane:** Add natural profanity sparingly for emphasis. Do not let profanity replace analysis.
- **Formal conversion:** If the user later asks for author or editor comments, translate every insult back into precise, constructive peer-review language.

Match the language used by the user. Keep the prose energetic and specific. Avoid a generic stream of insults; each punch should be attached to a verifiable defect.

## Hard Boundaries

- Attack the manuscript, not the authors as people.
- Do not target protected traits, identity, nationality, affiliation, career stage, or presumed intelligence.
- Do not allege fabrication, plagiarism, fraud, or misconduct without direct evidence.
- Do not invent defects, citations, experiments, quotations, or data problems.
- Do not threaten, harass, or encourage contact with the authors.
- Do not use dehumanizing or sexualized abuse.
- State uncertainty when the manuscript is ambiguous: use "appears," "is not demonstrated," or "cannot be determined from the description."
- If only an abstract or summary is available, say that the roast is provisional and limit it to the available evidence.

The goal is maximal rhetorical force with intact scientific judgment. A harsh conclusion must still survive a calm fact-check.
